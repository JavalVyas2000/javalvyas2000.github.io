GitHub

This project investigates the use of Large Language Models (LLMs) as autonomous agents capable of performing control tasks and handling faults in dynamic environments. Moving beyond static prompt-response roles, LLMs here are conceptualized as intelligent agents embedded in a multi-agent control loop, capable of monitoring plant conditions, issuing control commands, validating outcomes, and adapting strategies through reasoning.

Key Contributions

  • LLMs as Control Agents: The system leverages LLMs to act as real-time controllers, analyzing current sensor data and deciding control actions such as adjusting heater states in a thermoelectric system. These actions are then executed in simulation or on physical hardware.
  • LLMs as Fault Handling Operators: Inspired by industrial operator roles, the ETFA paper component demonstrates how LLMs can act during fault conditions — identifying abnormal behavior, proposing mitigation strategies, and steering the plant toward a safe recovery state.
  • Multi-Agent Framework: The architecture is structured around five intelligent agents:
    • Monitoring Agent: Observes system conditions, detects anomalies, and determines when to trigger fault handling.
    • Action Agent: Selects control commands based on goals, constraints, and system state.
    • Digital Twin: A simulation environment to verify and predict the effects of proposed actions.
    • Validation Agent: Evaluates simulated outcomes for constraint violations or unsafe behavior.
    • Reprompting Agent: Refines decisions through iterative reasoning when invalid outcomes are detected.
  • Physical Experiments with Temperature Control:
    • Relay Logic: Demonstrates simple temperature switching via on/off control.
    • Collaborative Control: Multiple LLM agents coordinate to maintain a setpoint temperature using reasoning and communication.
  • Fault Recovery Case Studies: In more advanced scenarios, the LLM operator adapts to simulated faults (e.g., blocked flow paths, sensor drift) by selecting alternate actions or constraints to keep the plant operational.
  • Challenges and Future Directions: The primary challenge remains inference latency and consistency. Ongoing work focuses on improving response times, incorporating retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), and fine-tuning strategies to reduce reprompting cycles.

The overarching goal is to establish whether foundation models can take on functional roles in autonomous control and fault mitigation — not just describing solutions, but enacting them through integrated reasoning, feedback, and execution.
